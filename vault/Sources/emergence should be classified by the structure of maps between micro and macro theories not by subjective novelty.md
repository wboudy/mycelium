## Key Takeaways

This paper cuts through the vagueness around "emergence" by replacing subjective notions like "novel" or "surprising" with precise structural relationships between micro and macro theories. Instead of asking whether higher-level properties are "unexpected," Carroll and Parola ask: what is the mathematical structure of the map between levels, and does it commute with time evolution?

The taxonomy is clean. Type-0 (featureless): a simple many-to-one map from micro to macro states with no subsystem decomposition. Type-1 (local): macro subsystems are composed of specific micro subsystems, with Type-1a being algorithmically simple (like thermodynamics from statistical mechanics) and Type-1b being algorithmically complex (the map exists but is practically incomputable). Type-2 (nonlocal): macro entities or dynamics are defined nonlocally from the micro perspective — they emerge from patterns that span many micro subsystems in ways that can't be decomposed locally. Type-3 (augmented): new ontological elements at the macro level that are simply absent from the micro theory — this is close to "strong emergence" and would imply fundamental physics is incomplete.

The key philosophical move is that Types 0-2 are all "weak" emergence — the macro dynamics are fully determined by the micro dynamics — but they differ in how feasible deduction is. Type-1b emergence (algorithmically complex maps) creates the *illusion* of strong emergence: the higher-level behavior is in principle derivable from the lower level, but the derivation is so complex that no finite agent could perform it. This is relevant to AI: when [[hierarchical recurrence at two timescales achieves deep reasoning that flat transformers and chain-of-thought cannot]], the reasoning behavior may be Type-1b emergent from the network weights — determined but incompressible.

Dennett's "real patterns" framing is endorsed: the ability to coarse-grain away enormous amounts of micro-state information while retaining predictive power at the macro level makes emergent entities genuinely "real" — they capture non-trivial structure in the micro theory.

## Claims

- **[normative]** Emergence should be classified by the mathematical structure of maps between theories (states and dynamics), not by subjective judgments about novelty or surprise (supports)
- **[definition]** Type-0 emergence is a many-to-one map from micro to macro without subsystem decomposition; Type-1 adds local subsystem correspondence; Type-2 allows nonlocal macro entities; Type-3 introduces genuinely new ontological elements (neutral)
- **[causal]** Type-1b (incompressible) emergence creates the appearance of strong emergence because the map from micro to macro is algorithmically complex, even though macro dynamics are fully determined by micro dynamics (supports)
- **[causal]** Strong (Type-3) emergence would require that fundamental physics predictions are incorrect when applied to composite systems like brains — a logically possible but empirically implausible position (supports)
- **[normative]** Emergent higher-level entities are "real" in Dennett's sense because the ability to predict from coarse-grained information despite discarding vast amounts of micro-state data reflects non-trivial structure (supports)
- **[causal]** The distinction between epistemic and ontological emergence depends on one's stance toward ontology — Dennett's real patterns provide a middle path where weak emergence can still yield ontologically real entities (supports)
